### Best attacks


**1. Bipartite Checkerboard Parity Collapse + Degree-Sum Boundary-Forcing Theorem**

*Why:* Grid graphs are inherently bipartite. Every Hamiltonian path alternates between color classes: each step flips (x+y) mod 2. For 1+1+1 decomposition with empirically-observed (even, odd) per-chain (nH, nV): each chain's parity is fixed (starts black, ends white, or vice versa). The two hubs are geometrically fused into a single point. By handshaking lemma on the reduced 'super-graph' where the two hubs collapse to one mega-vertex, forced degree constraints on boundary vs. interior vertices dictate that 2 chains MUST occupy the perimeter ring to balance the vertex-degree sum. Interior-only chains would over-saturate interior vertices' degree-2 constraints, forcing at least 2 chains outward.

  - 1. **Formalize the degree-sum argument**: For an m×n grid with two hubs fused, compute the degree of each vertex in the remaining (m×n − 2) grid. Boundary vertices have degree 2–3, interior degree 4. When 3 chains (each length K = mn/3) partition the vertices, the total edges used = 3(K − 1) = (mn − 3). Prove that if all 3 chains are interior-only, the sum of (degree × times-used) exceeds interior capacity, forcing overflow to boundary.
  - 2. **Connect to checkerboard parity**: Each chain alternates bipartite color. Two hubs (fused) have a single checkerboard color. Three chains starting/ending at this hub must balance color-class occupancy. Prove that interior-only chains cannot satisfy the simultaneous constraints: (a) equal alternation (every step flips color), (b) fixed hub color, (c) K = mn/3 fixed length. At least one chain must 'wrap around' the boundary to escape the interior parity bottleneck.
  - 3. **Construct explicit counterexample or proof sketch**: For 6×6 grid (K=12), place two chains in interior trying to cover all mn/3=12 cells each without boundary touch. Show that the 4th chain (if interior) cannot satisfy bipartite parity for the fixed hub vertex. Test on empirical 6×6 1+1+1 solutions to verify the forced perimeter occupancy is *not* just dominant but necessary.
  - 4. **Generalize to all m×n where mn ≡ 0 (mod 6)**: Prove that for any such grid, the degree-sum + bipartite-parity constraints force exactly 2 chains to the boundary. This reduces Q-WRAP from a geometric hypothesis to an algebraic/combinatorial theorem.

**2. Recover 2-Chain HC Order via Bipartite-Constrained Enumeration + Discrete CWF Validation**

*Why:* The core Q-TWIST blocker is the underdetermined inverse problem: recovering the 2-chain's per-panel Hamiltonian-circuit order from a fold sequence is under-constrained because the fold sequence only tracks mountain/valley assignment (1 bit per crease), not panel sequence (log₂(K!) bits). HOWEVER, the bipartite checkerboard structure of grid graphs provides a LATENT constraint: every step in the 2-chain HC must alternate color classes. This reduces the search space from K! permutations to at most ~2ᴷ distinct bipartite-respecting orders (far smaller). Additionally, requiring the pairwise loops (2-chain + 1-chain through hubs) to satisfy Tw = 0 via discrete Calugareanu-White-Fuller provides a second filtering criterion. Together, these constraints may make enumeration tractable and reveal whether ANY 2+1 fold is foldable.

  - 1. **Define bipartite-respecting 2-chain enumeration**: Treat the 2-chain as a path on the bipartite grid graph starting at one hub (color A) and ending at the other (color B, or same, depending on K parity). Generate all Hamiltonian paths of length K respecting the bipartite alternation. On 6×6 this is tractable (K=12, ~10³–10⁴ paths). Filter by: (a) connectivity to hubs, (b) non-overlap with the two confirmed 1-chains (from fold sequence, assumed deterministic in 1+1+1 case).
  - 2. **For each candidate 2-chain, compute pairwise-loop twist**: Form the closed pairwise loops: (2-chain + 1-chain₁ + hubs) and (2-chain + 1-chain₂ + hubs). Compute linking number Lk via Banchoff's arctan formula on the discrete path. Compute writhe Wr by summing signed turn-angles weighted by mountain/valley and checkerboard parity. Apply Lk = Tw + Wr; require Tw = 0 for both loops.
  - 3. **Count solutions and validate on 6×6 ground truth**: Enumerate 2-chain orders for a known valid 2+1 fold from your empirical dataset. Verify that the true 2-chain order is recovered and satisfies Tw=0 on both pairwise loops. If recovered order is unique, this validates the enumeration method. If multiple solutions exist, explore whether additional fold-sequence constraints (e.g., rigid-foldability at each vertex, crease order causality) eliminate spurious solutions.
  - 4. **Test on non-foldable 6×6 candidates**: If your generator produces candidate 2+1 folds labeled as non-foldable (or generate by SAT solver trying to violate Tw=0), verify that NONE of the bipartite-respecting 2-chain enumerations satisfy the pairwise-loop twist criterion. This grounds the Q-TWIST criterion empirically and identifies whether 2+1 is structurally rare or impossible.
  - 5. **Extend to 8×8 or 9×6**: If the method scales, test on larger grids. If enumeration becomes intractable or pattern breaks, revert to grid-size-specific analysis or NP-hardness argument (expected, given ASP-completeness of Hamiltonicity in grid graphs).

**3. Planar Separator Theorem + Nested Dissection: Formal Reduction of Interior via Boundary-Fixed Decomposition**

*Why:* Lipton-Tarjan planar separator theorem proves any n-vertex planar graph partitions into subgraphs via an O(√n)-size separator. For rectangular grids, the outermost perimeter ring is a natural separator. If you prove that ANY valid 3-chain decomposition MUST allocate 2 chains to lie strictly on this separator (not interior), then the remaining interior subgraph is a punctured grid whose minimum Hamiltonian path cover is smaller. This reduces the problem from 'find any 1+1+1 decomposition of mn cells' to 'find 2 non-crossing paths on perimeter ring, then 1 path on interior'. Nested dissection can recursively apply this to larger grids, providing a constructive proof framework for Q-WRAP and an algorithm for solving Q-TWIST.

  - 1. **Define the perimeter separator formally**: For an m×n grid, the outermost perimeter ring (boundary vertices + one inner layer) forms a separator. Prove (via Lipton-Tarjan) that it has size O(√(mn)) and that its removal leaves at most 2 connected components, each ≤ 2mn/3 vertices.
  - 2. **Allocate chains to separator**: Prove that for any 3-chain decomposition (1+1+1), at least 2 chains MUST use edges from the perimeter separator. Use a degree-parity argument (as in Attack 1) or a reachability argument (e.g., every interior chain must 'exit' through the separator at least once; since there are only 2 hubs and the separator separates them, at most 1 chain can be purely interior without exiting).
  - 3. **Construct interior sub-problem**: After fixing 2 chains to the boundary separator, remove their vertices from the grid. The remaining interior is either a smaller grid-like graph or a punctured rectangle. Use Keshavarz-Kohjerdi's algorithm (or brute-force for small grids) to determine if this interior admits a Hamiltonian path with fixed entry/exit points (where the 2 boundary chains touch the interior).
  - 4. **Prove interior uniqueness or small branch count**: If the interior path is unique (or has ≤2 solutions), the boundary-fixed reduction successfully constrains the problem. Test on 6×6: extract empirical 1+1+1 solutions, identify the 2 perimeter chains, remove them, verify the interior 3rd chain is the ONLY Hamiltonian path on the punctured interior graph with the inferred entry/exit points.
  - 5. **Implement nested dissection for constructive proof**: Recursively peel layers: decompose perimeter into 2 chains, recurse on interior. If this algorithm terminates with valid solutions that match your empirical data, you have a CONSTRUCTIVE PROOF of Q-WRAP (the boundary-wrap is forced by the algorithm's structure) and an algorithmic framework for solving Q-TWIST (apply interior Hamiltonicity check at each recursion level).

**5. Mountain-Valley Parity + Bipartite Alternation Synchronization as Mechanical Necessity**

*Why:* Parity phase-locking is a structural necessity, not just a numerical coincidence. If position and fold-type rhythms are incommensurate (different periods or phase shifts), the fold cannot be globally consistent. This is a graph-theoretic obstruction, more fundamental than twist algebra, and much faster to check.

  - Observe that grid graphs are bipartite (checkerboard). Every Hamiltonian path alternates colors: black→white→black. At each cell traversal, (x+y) mod 2 flips exactly once per edge.
